# Chapter 4 — Songbird

## Overview

Songbird is the branch of Unreal Reels that works with a *song* instead of a script. The distinction matters, because a song already carries its own clock. In Chapter 3 you built the clock yourself — you narrated beats and measured their durations. Here the clock is given: it lives in the waveform of a real recording, and your whole job is to read it accurately and never fight it. A song has a tempo, a downbeat, sections, and words sung at specific moments. Songbird's entire discipline is to extract that timing from the audio and let it govern every cut, caption, and camera move.

That single commitment — **the librosa beat grid is the master clock** — is what makes Songbird trustworthy. Every mode runs `analyze_audio.py` first, and it writes `beat_data.json`: the bpm, the beat and downbeat timestamps, the per-frame energy envelope, the sections, the total duration in frames. Nothing downstream invents timing. If the beat data and the music ever disagree, the music wins and you regenerate the data. You never hand-edit it. This is the same audio-first rule as the rest of the book, just handed to you rather than built.

Songbird has three modes, and they share this clock. The first is the **audiogram or lyric video** — captions and a waveform over a background, and it needs *no paid services at all*. The second is **lyric-match** — you take a music video whose visuals are lovely but drift from the words, and re-cut it so every lyric line lands on the frame that fits it. The third is **songbird-dance** — a character who actually dances *on* the beat. The rule that ties them together is worth memorizing now: **one beat is one lyric line.** In the free lyric mode and in lyric-match, a beat is a sung line, roughly five or six seconds. In dance mode the beats grow to ten or fifteen seconds — a whole dance phrase — but they are still cut to real downbeats, never guessed.

The second pillar, alongside the beat grid, is **forced alignment**. A beat grid tells you *where the beats are*; it does not tell you *when a word is sung*. For that, Songbird runs `align_lyrics_audio.py`, which uses faster-whisper to transcribe the vocal with word-level timestamps, then sequence-aligns your *known* lyrics onto those timestamps. A word Whisper mishears gets corrected back to the real word while keeping its real timing. This is what makes karaoke captions hit the vocal instead of drifting past on an even grid.

## Mode one: the free lyric / audiogram video ✅

This is the mode to learn first, because it costs nothing and teaches the whole clock. You bring two files: `song.wav` and `lyrics.txt` (one line per sung line, a blank line between stanzas, an optional `TITLE:` line). Everything else is free and local.

The flow is three steps. First, **analyze the track** ✅: `analyze_audio.py <audio.wav> --fps 30 -o beat_data.json` reads the waveform and writes the beat grid, sections, energy, and duration. Second, **align the lyrics to the vocal** ✅: `align_lyrics_audio.py lyrics.txt --audio audio.wav --beat-data beat_data.json -o lyrics.json` gives you word-level timing via faster-whisper. Third, **render** ✅: the muzak Remotion overlay draws karaoke captions with the current word highlighted, over an audiogram waveform driven by the actual audio. You preview with `npx remotion studio`, scrub to check the sync, and render with `npx remotion render`.

Two cautions carry from the source. Run the alignment step *locally*, because faster-whisper downloads its model from Hugging Face on first run; in a locked-down sandbox that download can be blocked and the model won't load. When it can't, you fall back to `align_lyrics.py`, a beat-grid seed that spreads lines evenly across the real grid — good enough to preview layout, but it does not know when a word is actually sung. Always record which one you used in `timing_source`, and re-run the Whisper pass before the final render whenever you can. And leave Whisper's `--vad` off for sung vocals, or a soft sung intro gets clipped; bump `--model small` or `medium` for heavily stylized singing.

## The kids' language-learning music video ✅

Here is the use case that makes mode one shine, and it is entirely free. A children's language-learning song is almost purpose-built for Songbird: the lyrics are simple, the vocabulary repeats, the diction is clear, and the whole point is that a learner reads the target-language word *at the exact moment it is sung*. That is precisely what forced alignment delivers.

Bring a clean recording — "Head, shoulders, knees and toes," a colors song, a counting rhyme — and its lyrics in the target language. Analyze the track, align the lyrics, and let the Remotion overlay put big, crisp captions in the target language on screen, each word lighting up as it is sung. Because the captions are drawn *on top* as sharp Remotion text and never baked into an image, they stay legible and correctly spelled in any script — Spanish, Mandarin, Arabic. Clear diction gives Whisper an easy time, so the alignment lands cleanly. The bright, repetitive visuals behind the waveform can be as simple as an energy-driven color gradient (free), and you only reach for generated stills if a specific line genuinely earns one. It is the most free-friendly, most immediately useful thing you can make with Songbird — and one beat is still one lyric line.

## Mode two: lyric-match — re-cut an existing video to its words

Sometimes the picture already exists and only the timing is wrong: a performance cut, an AI montage, archival footage whose images drift off the words. Lyric-match keeps the good visuals and makes them obey the lyrics. It is the sibling of the main reels engine, with one difference — unreal-reels *generates* every still from scratch, while lyric-match *starts from the existing video's frames* and only regrades and animates them.

The chain: analyze the audio (the same master clock) and align the lyrics, then extract frames from the source at about one frame per second with `extract_frames.sh`, so a frame's index is roughly its second in the song. `pick_stills.py` then takes, for each beat, the frames whose timestamp falls in that beat's window and keeps the *sharpest* one — measured by the variance of the Laplacian — copying the keeper to `stills/<beat_id>_v1.png`. Now the heart of the skill: for every beat you **open the chosen still and read its lyric line**, and write an `image_prompt` that preserves the frame's subject and pose while pushing expression and light toward what the lyric means. A human — or a vision model — looked at both the picture and the words and decided they belong together. Each still becomes a Minimax Hailuo image-to-video clip, requested at ten seconds and center-trimmed to the beat, then muxed under the original audio with captions on top. And the rule holds: **one beat = one lyric line = one source still = one Hailuo clip cut to the beat.**

## Mode three: songbird-dance — a character on the beat

The third mode is for songs where the dance *is* the story. It is built for Higgsfield Seedance 2.0, which takes an image plus an audio clip and generates motion locked to that audio. Two things change from lyric-match. First, the beats grow long — ten to fifteen seconds, a whole dance phrase, snapped to musical downbeats and never over Seedance's fifteen-second cap. Second, the prompt shape changes: because the dance, the look, and the danceability *are* the content, the prompt is mostly **character plus choreography plus camera**, with style as a short tag.

`build_dance_beatsheet.py` greedily cuts the song into ten-to-fifteen-second downbeat-aligned segments, cycles the character stills, and assigns each beat a distinct move and camera. `slice_beat_audio.sh` writes each beat's audio slice, and `generate_video_seedance.sh` feeds the still and that slice to Seedance. Write the prompt in order: character (one brief clause — the still locks identity), the dance move and how it hits the beat, one specific camera angle varied per beat, an explicit background (vagueness makes the model invent a spotlight stage), and one short style line ending in "no text, no extra characters." Then verify what the model most likes to bluff: test one segment and compare its motion-energy peaks to the beat grid. Energetic is not the same as on-beat.

## Worked example — a free lyric video, end to end

Drop `song.wav` and `lyrics.txt` into `examples/songbird-demo/`. Run `analyze_audio.py song.wav --fps 30 -o beat_data.json`; it reports, say, 96 bpm, four sections, and a duration in frames. Run `align_lyrics_audio.py lyrics.txt --audio song.wav --beat-data beat_data.json -o lyrics.json` locally, so faster-whisper can fetch its model, and confirm `timing_source` reads as forced-aligned, not a seed. Open the muzak overlay in `npx remotion studio`, scrub to the first chorus, and watch each word brighten as it is sung. Fix any timing by ear now — it is far cheaper than after a render. Then `npx remotion render`. You have made a lyric video with no paid service in the loop, the clock read straight from the waveform.
